# Extending LANs with Repeaters and Bridges

## Big picture

As a network grows, a single LAN is often no longer enough. Distance limits, performance bottlenecks, reliability concerns, and security needs make it more practical to divide the network into smaller LANs and then interconnect them.

The slides explain three device types that can be used when LANs need to be extended or interconnected:

- **Repeaters**
- **Bridges**
- **Routers**

In these notes, the focus is on **repeaters** and **bridges**.

## Why LANs need to be extended

A LAN may need to be extended for several reasons:

- stations may be physically too far apart for one LAN segment
- one large LAN may become unreliable, because one fault can affect many devices
- performance can degrade as more devices compete for the same shared medium
- some traffic may need to be separated for security reasons
- geographically separated groups of users may need to be linked together

The general design goal is to avoid one huge shared LAN and instead build **small interconnected LANs**.

## Repeaters

### What a repeater is

A repeater is used to overcome the **distance limitation** of a LAN.

Signals weaken as they travel. This weakening is called **attenuation**. Because of attenuation, each LAN technology has a practical maximum segment length. For example, the slides note that a 10Base5 LAN segment is limited to 500 metres.

When devices need to communicate beyond the allowed distance of a single segment, a repeater can be inserted between segments.

[[IMAGE: repeater_implementation.png]]

### What a repeater does

A repeater works at the physical signalling level. Its core job is simple:

- it receives bits on one segment
- it regenerates those bits as a fresh signal
- it forwards the regenerated bits onward

The repeater does **not** examine or interpret frame contents.

### Key characteristics of repeaters

- used to interconnect **identical LANs**
- the interconnected segments still behave like **one LAN**, not separate LANs
- do **not** process frames
- do **not** perform filtering
- forward **every frame**
- require **unique addressing across the whole extended LAN**

### Important implication

A repeater extends a LAN segment, but it does **not** create traffic isolation. If a frame appears on one side, it is regenerated and forwarded to the other side. In effect, all attached devices remain part of the same collision and broadcast environment.

## Bridges

### What a bridge is

A bridge is used to interconnect small LANs to create one larger bridged LAN.

Unlike a repeater, a bridge **does process frames**. It can connect LANs that use **similar or even different MAC protocols**, such as IEEE 802.3 and IEEE 802.5.

[[IMAGE: bridge_implementation.png]]

### Why bridges are preferred over one large LAN

The slides highlight several reasons why multiple smaller LANs connected by bridges are often better than a single large LAN.

#### Reliability

A failure can be contained to one smaller LAN instead of affecting every device in a single large shared network.

#### Performance

Smaller LANs usually give better performance to locally attached stations. This links to the **Principle of Locality of Reference**, which says that much of the traffic generated by users tends to stay local.

If devices that communicate often are placed on the same LAN, much of their traffic never needs to cross a bridge.

#### Security

On some LAN topologies, especially bus and wireless LANs, many stations can potentially observe all frames. A bridge can physically separate high-security users or sensitive traffic onto different LAN sections.

#### Geography

Bridges allow isolated clusters of stations to be linked using longer-distance communication links such as microwave or satellite connections.

## Bridge versus repeater

The difference is central to this topic.

### Repeater

- extends the physical reach of a LAN
- works with bits, not frames
- no filtering
- forwards everything
- joins LAN segments into one larger segment

### Bridge

- interconnects separate LANs
- works with frames
- can filter traffic
- forwards only frames that need to cross to another LAN
- reduces unnecessary traffic between LANs

## Core functions of a bridge

The slides describe two main bridge functions:

- **Store and Forward**
- **Routing and Addressing**

## Store and forward

A basic bridge operates in **promiscuous mode**, meaning it reads all frames on an attached LAN. It stores each frame temporarily, examines the destination address, and decides whether the frame should be forwarded to another LAN.

If the destination station is on a different connected LAN, the bridge retransmits the frame on the appropriate outgoing port. If not, the frame is not copied across.

This behaviour happens in both directions.

### Why store and forward matters

Because the bridge receives a full frame before forwarding it, it can:

- decide whether the frame is relevant to another LAN
- prevent unnecessary traffic from crossing between LANs
- connect LANs that may run at different speeds or carry different traffic loads

The slide summary explicitly notes that store-and-forward makes it possible to connect **fast, busy LANs to slower LANs**.

## Routing and addressing in a bridged LAN

Even in a bridged network, stations still communicate using **unique MAC addresses**.

The bridge uses the destination MAC address in a frame to decide whether the frame should be forwarded and, if so, through which port.

The slides describe two routing strategies:

- **Fixed routing**
- **Address learning**

## Fixed routing

In fixed routing, the bridge contains a manually prepared routing table. For each relevant source-destination relationship, the bridge knows which port should be used.

[[IMAGE: example_bridge_routing_table.png]]

### How fixed routing works

- a frame arrives at the bridge
- the bridge checks the destination MAC address
- it looks up that address in its routing table
- it forwards the frame to the correct outgoing port if needed

### Advantages of fixed routing

- simple concept
- low processing overhead

### Disadvantages of fixed routing

- becomes difficult to manage as the network grows
- needs manual updates when stations or bridges are added or removed
- can become very complicated when multiple bridges are present

Fixed routing is therefore easy to understand, but hard to maintain at scale.

## Address learning

Address learning is a more practical alternative. Instead of requiring manual configuration, the bridge learns where stations are by observing incoming frames.

Every incoming MAC frame contains a **source address**. Each LAN connects to one bridge port. By associating the source MAC address with the incoming port, the bridge can gradually build its own routing table.

### How address learning begins

At boot-up, the bridge starts with an empty table.

As frames arrive:

- if the destination address is **not** already known, the bridge records the **source MAC address and incoming port**, then broadcasts the frame to all other ports
- if the destination address **is** known, the bridge forwards the frame to the correct outgoing port

After enough traffic has been seen, the bridge reaches a **steady state** where its table is mostly complete and filtering becomes effective.

[[IMAGE: address_learning_process.png]]

### Why address learning is useful

- little or no manual intervention is needed
- the bridge automatically adapts to observed traffic
- forwarding becomes more selective over time
- unnecessary inter-LAN traffic is reduced

Bridges that work this way are called **transparent bridges**, because the stations themselves are unaware that the bridge exists.

## The looping problem in transparent bridging

Address learning works well, but it introduces a serious issue when **redundant bridges** are present.

Redundant bridges are often added for reliability. If one bridge fails, another path can keep the network connected. The downside is that redundant paths can create **loops**.

[[IMAGE: bridge_loops.png]]

### Why loops are dangerous

If a frame can circulate around multiple bridge paths, several bad things can happen:

- duplicate frames may appear on LAN segments
- bridges may keep forwarding copies repeatedly
- the same traffic may circulate indefinitely
- bandwidth is wasted and the network can become unstable

The diagram sequence in the slides shows how one transmitted frame can be copied by multiple bridges and then reappear repeatedly on connected LANs.

## Spanning tree and loop prevention

To prevent loops, graph theory is used to create a **spanning tree**.

A spanning tree is a version of the network graph with **no loops**.

### Representing the bridged network as a graph

The slides show the network represented as follows:

- **nodes** represent bridges and LANs
- **arcs** represent the physical connections between bridges and LANs
- each arc is assigned a cost metric

[[IMAGE: example_bridged_network.png]]

### Steps for finding the spanning tree

The slides give a three-step process.

#### Identify the root bridge

The bridge with the **lowest bridge ID** is chosen as the **root bridge**.

#### Find the shortest paths

Find the shortest path from the root bridge to every other bridge and LAN in the network.

[[IMAGE: finding_the_shortest_path.png]]

#### Eliminate redundant routes

Only the routes that belong to the shortest-path structure remain active in the spanning tree. The others are disabled for forwarding.

### Forwarding ports and blocking ports

All bridge ports that belong to the spanning tree become **forwarding ports**.

All other ports become **blocking ports**.

A blocking port remains physically connected but does not forward frames, which prevents loops while still preserving redundancy in case the topology later needs to change.

## Performance benefits of bridges

Bridges improve performance in several important ways.

### Parallelism

Not every frame arriving at a bridge is copied to another LAN. This means stations on separate LAN segments can communicate at the same time, provided their traffic does not need to contend on the same segment.

In other words, two separate conversations can occur in parallel on different LANs.

[[IMAGE: optimized_performance_and_parallelism.png]]

The slide also gives a simple intuition:

Without bridging, each station effectively perceives the LAN speed as roughly:

`LAN data rate / number of stations`

With bridging, that contention is reduced because traffic is split across smaller LAN sections.

### Better locality

If stations that frequently communicate are placed on the same LAN, their traffic stays local. The bridge only forwards traffic that truly needs to move to another LAN.

This matches the Principle of Locality of Reference and improves overall efficiency.

### Smaller collision domains

One large shared LAN has one larger collision domain. Bridging breaks the network into smaller collision domains.

[[IMAGE: reduced_collision_domains_with_bridging.png]]

Because collisions are not copied through a bridge, contention is reduced and performance improves.

## Exam-style distinctions to remember

### Repeater versus bridge

**Repeater**
- physical-layer style device
- regenerates bits
- extends distance
- forwards everything
- no frame filtering

**Bridge**
- data-link aware device
- reads and forwards frames
- filters traffic based on MAC addresses
- can connect similar or different MAC protocol LANs
- reduces unnecessary traffic and collisions between LANs

### Fixed routing versus address learning

**Fixed routing**
- manual routing table
- simple but hard to scale

**Address learning**
- automatic table building
- more flexible
- basis of transparent bridging

### Forwarding port versus blocking port

**Forwarding port**
- actively forwards frames as part of the spanning tree

**Blocking port**
- stays inactive for forwarding to prevent loops

## Final summary

Repeaters and bridges are both used to extend LANs, but they solve very different problems.

A **repeater** solves a physical-distance problem. It regenerates bits and extends a LAN segment without understanding frames.

A **bridge** solves a traffic-organisation problem. It interconnects smaller LANs, processes frames, learns or stores routing information, and forwards only relevant traffic. This improves reliability, security, locality, parallelism, and collision behaviour.

The biggest challenge for bridges is **looping** when redundant paths exist. That is handled by constructing a **spanning tree**, choosing a root bridge, finding shortest paths, and placing non-essential ports into a blocking state.

## Quick review questions

**Why is a repeater not considered to connect two separate LANs?**  
Because it simply regenerates and forwards bits, so the connected segments still behave as one LAN.

**Why does a bridge improve performance?**  
Because it filters traffic, keeps local traffic local, allows parallel communication across separate LANs, and reduces collision-domain size.

**What information does a bridge use to learn station location automatically?**  
The source MAC address in incoming frames and the port on which the frame arrived.

**Why are redundant bridges useful but risky?**  
They improve reliability, but they can create loops unless a spanning-tree mechanism blocks some ports.

**What is the purpose of a blocking port?**  
To keep the network loop-free by preventing forwarding on redundant paths.
