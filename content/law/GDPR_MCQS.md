# GDPR Expected Exam MCQ Bank


# Section 1: Privacy Before GDPR and Irish Privacy Law

**Exam likelihood: 48/50**

## MCQ 1

Which article is strongly associated with the early modern idea of a legal right to privacy?

A. The Right to Property, 1789  
B. The Right to Privacy, Harvard Law Review, 1890  
C. The Right to Expression, 1950  
D. The Right to Data Protection, 1995  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Warren and Brandeis published *The Right to Privacy* in the Harvard Law Review in 1890. It is a key starting point for modern privacy theory.

## MCQ 2

What phrase is commonly linked to Mr Justice Cooley and the early concept of privacy?

A. The right to process data  
B. The right to be forgotten  
C. The right to be left alone  
D. The right to public expression  

**Answer: C**

**Explanation:** The phrase "the right to be left alone" became a central idea in early privacy law.

## MCQ 3

What was the main complaint made by Warren and Brandeis about the press?

A. The press was too controlled by government  
B. The press was intruding into private domestic life  
C. The press refused to publish political opinions  
D. The press was collecting taxes  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Their article criticised newspapers for spreading gossip and intruding into private life.

## MCQ 4

Which Irish constitutional article protects the personal rights of the citizen?

A. Article 40.3  
B. Article 41.3.1  
C. Article 40.5  
D. Article 8  

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** Article 40.3 states that the State guarantees to respect and defend the personal rights of the citizen.

## MCQ 5

Which Irish constitutional article protects the dwelling of the citizen?

A. Article 40.3  
B. Article 40.5  
C. Article 41.3.1  
D. Article 51  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Article 40.5 states that the dwelling of every citizen is inviolable and cannot be forcibly entered except according to law.

## MCQ 6

Which case is associated with marital privacy in Ireland?

A. Kennedy and Arnold v Ireland  
B. McGee v Attorney General  
C. DPP v McCann  
D. National Irish Banks v RTE  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** *McGee v Attorney General* recognised marital privacy as an important constitutional privacy matter.

## MCQ 7

Which case is associated with communication privacy and phone tapping?

A. Kennedy and Arnold v Ireland  
B. Bailey v Flood  
C. Douglas v Hello!  
D. Campbell v Mirror Group Newspapers  

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** *Kennedy and Arnold v Ireland* involved phone tapping and recognised privacy in phone conversations.

## MCQ 8

In Kennedy and Arnold v Ireland, the court treated privacy as what type of right?

A. A property right only  
B. An unenumerated constitutional right  
C. A commercial contract right  
D. A right held only by companies  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** The right to privacy was recognised as an unenumerated personal right flowing from the nature of the State.

## MCQ 9

Which statement best describes privacy under Irish constitutional law?

A. It is absolute and can never be limited  
B. It exists but may be limited by the common good  
C. It applies only to companies  
D. It applies only to media publications  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Irish privacy rights are important but not absolute. They can be balanced against the common good and other rights.

## MCQ 10

Which case involved privacy being balanced against the right to life?

A. DPP v McCann  
B. McGee v Attorney General  
C. Douglas v Hello!  
D. Ahearne v RTE  

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** *DPP v McCann* involved a conflict where the right to privacy was treated as inferior to the right to life.

## MCQ 11

Which case involved privacy being balanced against freedom of expression and public interest in exposing wrongdoing?

A. National Irish Banks v RTE  
B. McGrory v ESB  
C. Jane O'Keefe v Ryanair  
D. Redmond v Flood  

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** *National Irish Banks v RTE* involved privacy, confidentiality, freedom of expression, and public interest.

## MCQ 12

What does the idea that privacy is "not absolute" mean?

A. Privacy does not exist in law  
B. Privacy can be limited when stronger public or constitutional interests exist  
C. Privacy applies only online  
D. Privacy applies only to famous people  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Privacy may yield to the common good, public safety, freedom of expression, right to life, or other important interests.

## MCQ 13

Which case is linked to privacy and medical information in a personal injuries claim?

A. McGrory v ESB  
B. Campbell v Mirror Group Newspapers  
C. Kennedy and Arnold v Ireland  
D. Norris v Attorney General  

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** In *McGrory v ESB*, the defendant was entitled to enquire into the plaintiff's medical condition because it was relevant to the claim.

## MCQ 14

Which case is associated with privacy as property or commercial control over wedding photographs?

A. Douglas v Hello!  
B. McGee v Attorney General  
C. Haughey v Moriarty  
D. Bailey v Flood  

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** *Douglas v Hello!* involved paid rights to wedding photographs and was framed more as confidential information than a pure privacy right.

## MCQ 15

What was one key point from Ahearne and Ahearne v RTE?

A. All people in a story always have identical privacy rights  
B. Different people may have different privacy expectations depending on their role  
C. Privacy never applies to nursing homes  
D. Media freedom always wins  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** The case shows that privacy analysis may differ for nurses, patients, owners, or other affected categories.

## MCQ 16

Which case is associated with the idea that privacy may yield to the maintenance of public morals?

A. Norris v Attorney General  
B. McGee v Attorney General  
C. Kennedy and Arnold v Ireland  
D. DPP v McCann  

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** In *Norris v Attorney General*, the Supreme Court refused to recognise absolute individual privacy.

## MCQ 17

The Irish Data Protection Acts 1988 to 2003 were later what?

A. Strengthened but left untouched  
B. Superseded by later data protection law  
C. Applied only to newspapers  
D. Used only for criminal trials  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** The notes state that the Data Protection Acts 1988 to 2003 were superseded.

## MCQ 18

Which of the following is the best exam summary of privacy before GDPR?

A. Privacy had no legal recognition before GDPR  
B. Privacy existed through constitutional law, case law, and earlier data protection law, but GDPR expanded and systematised data protection

D. Privacy applied only to state bodies  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** GDPR did not invent privacy. It built on older privacy and data protection developments.

## MCQ 28

# Section 4: Core GDPR Definitions

**Exam likelihood: 50/50**

## MCQ 37

Who is a data controller?

A. A person or body that determines the purposes and means of processing personal data  
B. Any person whose data is processed  
C. A person who only stores data for personal use  
D. A person who investigates criminal offences only  

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** The controller decides why and how personal data is processed.

## MCQ 38

Who is a data processor?

A. The person whose data is being processed  
B. A body that processes personal data on behalf of the controller  
C. A regulator that fines companies  
D. A court that hears privacy cases  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** A processor acts on behalf of the controller.

## MCQ 39

Who is a data subject?

A. A company whose accounts are processed  
B. An identified or identifiable natural person  
C. A database table  
D. A public authority only  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** A data subject must be a natural person, not a company or non-human entity.

## MCQ 40

Can a corporation be a data subject under GDPR?

A. Yes, always  
B. Yes, if it is based in the EU  
C. No, a data subject must be a natural person  
D. Only if it gives consent  

**Answer: C**

**Explanation:** GDPR protects personal data of natural persons. Corporate information is not personal data unless it relates to identifiable individuals.

## MCQ 41

Which of the following is most likely to be personal data?

A. A person's name and location data  
B. A completely anonymous weather statistic  
C. A company's general office address with no individual named  
D. A blank form template  

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** A name and location data can identify or help identify a natural person.

## MCQ 42

Which of the following can be an identifier under GDPR?

A. Name  
B. Identification number  
C. Online identifier  
D. All of the above  

**Answer: D**

**Explanation:** GDPR includes names, numbers, location data, online identifiers, and other identity factors.

## MCQ 43

What is biometric data?

B. Data relating to physical, physiological, or behavioural characteristics used for unique identification  
C. Data about website traffic only

## MCQ 44

Which is an example of biometric data?

# Section 5: GDPR Principles under Article 5.1

**Exam likelihood: 50/50**

## MCQ 59

Which principle requires personal data to be processed in a way that is lawful, fair, and open to the data subject?

A. Storage limitation  
B. Transparency, fairness, and lawfulness  
C. Accuracy only  
D. Portability  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** The first major principle is lawful, fair, and transparent processing.

## MCQ 60

Which principle says personal data should be collected only for specified, explicit, and legitimate purposes?

A. Purpose limitation  
B. Accountability  
C. Portability  
D. Encryption  

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** Purpose limitation restricts why data may be collected and used.

## MCQ 61

Which principle requires personal data to be adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary?

A. Data minimisation  
B. Public interest  
C. Processing expansion  
D. Profiling  

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** Data minimisation means do not collect or process more personal data than needed.

## MCQ 62

Which principle requires personal data to be accurate and kept up to date?

A. Integrity  
B. Accuracy  
C. Consent  
D. Main establishment  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Accuracy requires reasonable steps to keep personal data correct and current.

## MCQ 63

Which principle limits how long personal data may identify a data subject?

A. Storage limitation  
B. Freedom of expression  
C. Cross-border processing  
D. Consent withdrawal  

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** Storage limitation means data should not identify people longer than necessary for processing.

## MCQ 64

Which principle requires data to be processed securely?

A. Integrity and confidentiality  
B. Filing system  
C. Legitimate interest only  
D. Public authority  

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** Security of personal data is often described as integrity and confidentiality.

## MCQ 65

A company collects customers' passport numbers for a newsletter sign-up. Which principle is most obviously at risk?

A. Data minimisation  
B. Main establishment  
C. Data portability  
D. Freedom of expression  

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** Passport numbers are not normally necessary for a newsletter.

## MCQ 66

A company collects data for account creation, then later uses it for unrelated advertising without warning. Which principle is most clearly breached?

A. Purpose limitation  
B. Filing system  
C. Article 8 ECHR  
D. DPO service contract  

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** Data collected for one clear purpose should not be reused for an incompatible hidden purpose.

## MCQ 67

A company keeps customer records forever without justification. Which principle is most relevant?

A. Storage limitation  
B. Cross-border processing  
C. Consent by silence  
D. EU adequacy  

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** Data should be kept in identifiable form only as long as needed.

## MCQ 68

A company stores personal data in an unsecured spreadsheet accessible to all staff. Which principle is most relevant?

A. Security, integrity, and confidentiality  
B. Data portability  
C. Privacy as property  
D. Marital privacy  

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** Poor access control creates a security and confidentiality issue.

## MCQ 69

Why are the Article 5.1 principles exam-important?

A. They are optional guidelines only  
B. They summarise the core duties for handling personal data  
C. They apply only to newspapers  
D. They apply only outside the EU  

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** The principles are the backbone of GDPR compliance.

## MCQ 70

Which set best matches the GDPR principles listed in the notes?

A. Lawfulness, fairness, transparency, purpose limitation, data minimisation, accuracy, storage limitation, security  
B. Profit, publicity, ownership, secrecy, delay

# Final High-Priority Revision List

Memorise these first:

1. Data controller means the person or body deciding purposes and means of processing.
2. Data processor means the person or body processing on behalf of the controller.
3. Data subject means an identified or identifiable natural person.
4. Consent must be freely given, specific, informed, unambiguous, and affirmative.
5. Silence, inactivity, and pre-ticked boxes are not valid consent.
6. GDPR principles: lawfulness, fairness, transparency, purpose limitation, data minimisation, accuracy, storage limitation, security.
7. Data portability requires structured, commonly used, machine-readable format.
8. The right to be forgotten means erasure in certain circumstances.
9. Article 6 covers lawful bases for processing.
10. Special category data is generally forbidden to process unless a specific exception applies.
11. DPIAs are required for high-risk processing.
12. Data protection by design means privacy must be built in from the start.
13. Data protection by default means protective settings and minimum necessary data by default.
14. Breach notification is generally required within 72 hours of awareness.
15. Data subjects must be notified without undue delay where there is high risk to their rights and freedoms.
16. Encryption can reduce risks and may affect notification duties.
17. Controllers must ensure processors provide sufficient guarantees.
18. A processor cannot freely appoint a sub-processor without authorisation.
19. DPOs are required for public authorities, large-scale monitoring, or large-scale special category processing.
20. International transfers must not undermine GDPR protections.
21. Binding Corporate Rules must protect EU data subjects and be approved by the DPA.
22. IP addresses and cookies may be personal data because they can act as online identifiers.
23. Fines can reach 20 million euro or 4 percent of global annual turnover, whichever is greater.
24. A data audit is the first practical step toward compliance.
25. Documentation is vital evidence of compliance.
